In the insulated case (zero external heat flux), the solid wall should be nearly isothermal through its thickness in steady state — and the data confirms this precisely. The temperature difference between the inner wall (fluid-solid interface) and outer wall (solid exterior) is 0.70 K maximum for the legacy reducer and just 0.06 K for the new reducer. These values are negligible compared to the fluid-side temperature variation (~18 K and ~18 K respectively across the domain). The Biot number Bi = hL/k_s ≈ 0.003–0.004 means the conductive resistance through the solid is approximately 250× smaller than the external convective resistance. This directly validates the CHT modelling assumption and confirms that the thermal performance difference between the two geometries is driven entirely by external surface area — not by any difference in through-wall conduction.

Redesigned quintic reducer

Insulation effect: +64.6 K at inlet, +56.2 K at outlet

New: insulated 671.6→653.8K vs uninsulated 607→597.6K. +65K insulation effect — 2.5× legacy.

Both cases use identical outlet boundary conditions (0 Pa gauge) so this is a clean comparison of the insulation effect alone. The insulated wall is consistently warmer because no heat escapes externally — the gas retains its thermal energy through the contraction. The legacy reducer shows a +25 K insulation effect; the new quintic reducer shows a +65 K effect, 2.5× larger. This directly quantifies the external surface area penalty of the redesigned geometry: the 150 mm quintic contraction exposes approximately 8× more wall area to ambient, so eliminating external convection has a far greater impact on the new reducer's wall temperature. Critically, the insulated new reducer achieves a higher outlet wall temperature (653.8 K) than the insulated legacy reducer (648.1 K), confirming the outlet temperature reversal reported in the thesis. Under insulated conditions, the new reducer's superior pressure recovery translates into a small but definite thermal advantage — the external area penalty is the only reason this advantage is obscured in the uninsulated CHT cases.
