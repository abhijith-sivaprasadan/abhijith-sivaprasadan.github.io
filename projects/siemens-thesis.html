Siemens Energy CFD/CHT Thesis - Gas Turbine Reducer Thermo-Fluid Analysis

Industrial context

This thesis was completed in the Fluid Dynamic Lab at Siemens Energy Finspång as part of the Pulsatorn programme, a high-temperature rig used for dynamic pressure sensor calibration in gas-turbine-relevant conditions. The assessed component was the reducer between heater outlet and downstream test section. In the existing hardware, the reducer used a commercial two-step contraction. A redesign based on a 150 mm quintic C²-continuous profile was proposed to reduce abrupt area changes and improve flow quality.

Problem definition

The decision question was not only aerodynamic. The redesigned reducer was expected to reduce internal losses, but it also had a larger external surface area that could increase heat rejection to ambient. The task was therefore to quantify both effects under consistent operating conditions and produce a decision-ready recommendation for rig use. Planned experimental validation was reduced after heater failure during test preparation, so the final scope became a supervisor-approved numerical thesis with explicit limitation tracking.

What I did

I developed a steady-state compressible CFD/CHT workflow in ANSYS Fluent using ideal-gas air and k-omega SST with enhanced near-wall treatment. The model set was split into two studies: adiabatic CFD for flow-behaviour comparison and mesh independence, then conjugate heat transfer for thermal-delivery comparison. Simulations were executed for both geometries at outlet gauge pressures of 80 kPa, 40 kPa and 0 kPa, with inlet conditions of T = 673 K and P_gauge = 100 kPa. Quarter-symmetry was used for computational efficiency, and integral values were mapped back to full-geometry equivalents for reporting.

Mesh and setup quality were controlled through a three-level grid sweep. Baseline meshes contained 829,621 fluid cells for the legacy case and 913,301 for the redesigned case; key KPIs remained within a 2% variation band across coarse-baseline-fine levels, supporting mesh independence for engineering interpretation. I used Siemens NX and Teamcenter context for geometry and data handling, and structured the outputs around pressure-loss and thermal KPIs rather than single-point plots.

Geometry overview

Validation and measurement-chain link

In parallel with simulation work, I commissioned NI-DAQ measurement channels and modified an existing LabVIEW VI for synchronised acquisition of thermocouple and pressure data. Although sustained hot testing was interrupted by heater failure, this instrumentation work ensured the thesis stayed coupled to practical validation logic rather than becoming a detached simulation exercise. The failure event was documented through formal root-cause analysis, and the approved scope revision was reflected in methods and conclusions.

Outcomes

The redesigned reducer showed the stronger internal flow behaviour and supported near-choked outlet conditions (Ma around 0.989-0.990) compared with supersonic tendency in the legacy profile (Ma up to 1.016 adiabatic, 1.006 in CHT). Thermal interpretation, however, required area effects: the redesigned geometry presented a 3.35x larger exposed surface, which increased ambient heat-loss opportunity in uninsulated operation. Under insulated conditions, the redesigned option recovered and surpassed legacy thermal delivery, with a +1.24 K outlet-temperature advantage at Case C.

The engineering conclusion is conditional and actionable: adopt the redesigned reducer with insulation to capture aerodynamic gains without thermal penalty. If insulation is not feasible, the legacy geometry can remain thermally competitive only because of lower exposed area, not better internal flow behaviour.

What I would improve next

The next step is a closed simulation-to-test loop: re-run high-temperature validation with the repaired heater chain, include external insulation as a controlled variable, and add transient pulsation and radiation effects to narrow uncertainty margins. That extension would convert the current decision logic from validated numerical recommendation to experimentally confirmed design guidance for future rig configurations.

Relevance

Why this matters

This work covers the full simulation-to-hardware loop: geometry preparation in SpaceClaim, mesh design and independence verification in ANSYS Meshing, compressible RANS and CHT solving in Fluent, interpretation through Biot number analysis, measurement-chain commissioning in NI-DAQ and LabVIEW, and documentation of assumptions and limitations. It is the strongest evidence on the site for gas turbine CFD, test engineering and high-temperature hardware roles.

The practical takeaway is also direct: if the redesigned reducer is used in the rig, it should be paired with external insulation. That preserves the aerodynamic improvement while removing the thermal penalty from the larger surface area.
