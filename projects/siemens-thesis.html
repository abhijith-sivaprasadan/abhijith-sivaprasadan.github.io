Siemens Energy CFD/CHT Thesis - Gas Turbine Reducer Thermo-Fluid Analysis

The rig and the problem

The Pulsatorn rig at Siemens Energy Finspång is a high-temperature dynamic pressure sensor calibration system for gas-turbine applications. The reducer between the heater and the downstream test section was identified as a likely source of both aerodynamic loss and unwanted heat dissipation. The legacy reducer is a commercially available hydraulic fitting with two abrupt contractions, while the redesigned reducer uses a quintic C²-continuous contraction to remove the step changes.

The engineering question was straightforward: does the smoother reducer improve flow quality enough to justify its larger body, and what is the thermal penalty if it is left uninsulated? The thesis was initially meant to include experimental rig testing, but heater failure forced a scope revision to a controlled numerical study approved by both supervisors.

CFD and CHT methodology

I built steady-state compressible RANS models in ANSYS Fluent using k-omega SST turbulence closure and ideal-gas air. Study 1 used adiabatic CFD across three mesh levels to establish mesh independence; Study 2 used six CHT simulations at outlet gauge pressures of 80 kPa, 40 kPa and 0 kPa for both geometries. Quarter-symmetry was used throughout, and all integral outputs were corrected back to full-geometry values.

The baseline meshes contained 829,621 fluid cells for the legacy reducer and 913,301 for the redesigned reducer. Across the coarse, baseline and fine grids, the monitored KPIs changed by less than 2%, so the mesh independence criterion was met. At the supercritical case, the redesigned reducer converged to Ma = 0.989-0.990, while the legacy reducer converged to Ma = 1.010-1.016 in the adiabatic study and Ma = 1.006 in CHT.

Geometry overview

Key findings

The redesigned reducer had the better internal flow field and lower heat-flux intensity per unit external area, but its longer body exposed 3.35 times more surface area to ambient. That meant the legacy reducer delivered hotter gas under uninsulated operation, while the redesigned reducer won once external heat loss was removed. At Case C, the redesigned geometry recovered the thermal gap and delivered 1.24 K hotter gas under insulated conditions.

The geometric trade-off was therefore clear. The redesigned reducer is aerodynamically preferable and supports near-choked flow at the geometric outlet, but it only realises that advantage thermally if external insulation is added. The legacy geometry remains thermally advantaged only because it is physically smaller.

Instrumentation and scope revision

In parallel with the modelling work, I commissioned the NI-DAQ measurement chain for the rig and adapted an existing LabVIEW VI for synchronised logging of thermocouple, pressure and sensor channels. During preliminary thermal testing the heater assembly failed, preventing sustained experimental operation. I documented the failure, supported the scope revision to a purely numerical thesis, and aligned the final work with the approved supervisor change.

The thesis also records the limitations clearly: steady-state only, no radiation, no experimental validation of the redesigned reducer, and no transient pulsation modelling. Those limitations are not hidden; they are part of the technical argument and the future-work plan.

Relevance

Why this matters

This work covers the full simulation-to-hardware loop: geometry preparation in SpaceClaim, mesh design and independence verification in ANSYS Meshing, compressible RANS and CHT solving in Fluent, interpretation through Biot number analysis, measurement-chain commissioning in NI-DAQ and LabVIEW, and documentation of assumptions and limitations. It is the strongest evidence on the site for gas turbine CFD, test engineering and high-temperature hardware roles.

The practical takeaway is also direct: if the redesigned reducer is used in the rig, it should be paired with external insulation. That preserves the aerodynamic improvement while removing the thermal penalty from the larger surface area.
