Siemens Energy CFD/CHT Thesis - Gas Turbine Reducer Thermo-Fluid Analysis

Seven months embedded at Siemens Energy Finspång conducting compressible CFD and conjugate heat transfer analysis of reducer geometries for the Pulsatorn high-temperature dynamic pressure sensor calibration rig.

ANSYS FluentCFD/CHTNI-DAQLabVIEWSiemens NX

Project snapshot

  • Organisation: Siemens Energy AB, Fluid Dynamic Lab, Finspång
  • Duration: 7 months (master’s thesis)
  • Simulation: ANSYS Fluent, ANSYS Meshing, ANSYS SpaceClaim
  • Turbulence model: k-omega SST, enhanced near-wall treatment
  • Instrumentation: NI-DAQ, NI MAX, LabVIEW VI
  • PLM: Siemens NX, Teamcenter PLM2020
  • Inlet conditions: T = 673 K, P_gauge = 100 kPa
  • Campaign: one thesis CFD/CHT campaign with adiabatic and full CHT variants
  • Biot number: Bi = 0.003–0.004 (lumped regime)

The rig and the problem

The Pulsatorn calibration rig at Siemens Energy Finspång conditions high-temperature, high-pressure air before it reaches the face of a dynamic pressure sensor used to characterise gas-turbine hot-section pulsation. Three reducer geometries sit immediately upstream of the sensor and determine the local flow regime and thermal environment at the measurement point. My task was to build CFD and conjugate heat transfer models of all three reducers, compare their pressure-loss, Mach-number distribution and thermal delivery, and determine whether the sensor-tip environment would remain within design limits.

CFD and CHT methodology

I built steady-state compressible RANS models in ANSYS Fluent using k-omega SST turbulence closure with enhanced wall treatment. Inlet conditions were T = 673 K and P_gauge = 100 kPa. For each reducer I ran two variants: an adiabatic case to isolate flow-regime behaviour — pressure loss, velocity field and vena contracta location — and a full conjugate heat transfer case that coupled the fluid domain to the solid reducer wall to resolve the temperature field through the metal and predict the thermal environment reaching the sensor.

Before the full campaign I conducted a three-level mesh independence study on the baseline geometry using prismatic inflation layers for near-wall resolution. Pressure-loss coefficient and peak Mach number both changed by less than 2% between the medium and fine meshes, confirming discretisation independence. Two geometries exhibited near-choked conditions at the vena contracta; one showed a localised supersonic acceleration followed by a weak normal shock — a flow regime that was not visible from pressure-loss coefficients alone and required Mach-number contour inspection to diagnose.

Thermal analysis — Biot number and redesign comparison

Biot number analysis yielded Bi = 0.003–0.004, placing all three reducers firmly in the lumped-capacitance regime: internal temperature gradients within the metal are negligible compared to the surface-to-fluid temperature difference. This result allowed me to attribute temperature differences between the baseline and redesigned geometries primarily to flow-regime variation rather than to differences in conductive resistance, giving a cleaner basis for the redesign comparison.

Instrumentation and root cause analysis

In parallel with the simulation work I commissioned the NI-DAQ measurement chain for the rig: J- and K-type thermocouple channels, a static pressure channel, and the Pulsatorn dynamic pressure sensor itself. I modified an existing LabVIEW VI to synchronise and log all channels at the required acquisition rate. During preliminary thermal testing at temperatures up to 700°C a heater element failed; I led the root cause investigation, traced the failure to thermal-cycling fatigue at a weld joint, and formally documented the finding and remediation recommendations for engineering review.

All domain choices, boundary conditions, turbulence model settings, assumptions and limitations were formally documented in the thesis submission, with next-step validation needs defined for transient and pulsating operation.

Relevance

Why this matters

This work covers the full simulation-to-hardware loop: geometry preparation in SpaceClaim, mesh design and independence verification in ANSYS Meshing, compressible RANS and CHT solving in Fluent, physical interpretation through Biot number analysis, measurement-chain commissioning in NI-DAQ and LabVIEW, and engineering-grade documentation of assumptions and limitations. The combined CFD and instrumentation scope maps directly onto gas turbine CFD, test engineering and high-temperature hardware roles. The root cause analysis experience and formal validation-planning methodology are additional differentiators for roles where numerical models need to be grounded in real rig data.
