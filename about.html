About

Thermal-fluid researcher and industrial R&D engineer based in Solna, Sweden.

Abhijith is an M.Sc. Sustainable Energy Engineering candidate at KTH Royal Institute of Technology with embedded Siemens Energy thesis work in compressible CFD/CHT, high-temperature instrumentation and experimental-numerical validation. The same profile also covers industrial decarbonization methodology, energy/process modelling, Python-based optimisation and reproducible engineering tools for decision support.

Current snapshot

Current technical direction

My current focus has two connected tracks. The research track is the thermal-fluid mechanics of high-temperature flow systems: how deposit formation and surface condition change heat transfer behaviour, how CHT models can be connected to measurable rig quantities, and how monitoring indicators can be derived from simulation and measurement data. The industrial R&D track is decarbonization and electrification: energy/process modelling, optimisation, techno-economic reasoning, prototype tools and decision-support methods for industrial utilities such as electrical systems, compressed air, heating and cooling.

  • Current location: Solna, Sweden
  • Primary differentiator: Siemens Energy Finspång thesis on a high-temperature dynamic pressure sensor calibration rig
  • Target roles: doctoral research in thermal-fluid systems and Associate Scientist / industrial R&D in decarbonization and electrification

Role fit

Why these two directions belong together

PhD / research direction

The Siemens thesis formed a research identity around high-temperature passages, surface-condition effects, thermal resistance decomposition and the limits of steady-state CFD/CHT when hardware behaviour becomes the real constraint.

Associate Scientist / industrial R&D direction

The industrial energy work shows method development beyond simulation: load-driver regression, metering-gap assessment, deviation detection, dispatch optimisation and documented outputs for industrial decarbonization decisions.

Shared working pattern

The common thread is disciplined evidence: define the technical question, build a model or measurement chain, test assumptions, explain uncertainty and translate the result into a decision someone can act on.

Working style

Validate assumptions early

Prefer clear boundary conditions, documented limitations and sanity checks before scaling a model or a test setup.

Connect analysis to hardware

Keep instrumentation, test constraints and model interpretation tied together instead of treating simulation as a standalone exercise.

Move between disciplines

Switch between thermal-fluid engineering, energy systems and software implementation while keeping the same standard of evidence, traceability and technical communication.
