Alleima · Energy efficiency · Comparative audit methods

Energy Efficiency Intern - Alleima AB

Industrial energy-performance mapping, KPI/EnPI development, metering-readiness assessment and comparative audit-method framing across global sites.

Role snapshot

  • Organisation: Alleima AB · Tube EHS
  • Period: Dec 2024 – Jun 2025
  • Mode: Remote / Sandviken, Sweden
  • Brief: harmonise energy-audit practice across Alleima's global manufacturing sites using Sweden's EKL framework + Alleima's internal ENV1 standard as the baseline
  • Sites in scope: Sandviken (SE), Chomutov (CZ), Werther (DE), Venlo (NL), Mehsana (IN), Zhenjiang (CN), Kennewick + Scranton (US)
  • Deliverable: 39-page consultancy report — "Global Adaptation of Swedish Energy Audit Frameworks: A Comparative Study Across Manufacturing Sites"

What I contributed

  • Built a comparative regulatory matrix across 7 jurisdictions (Sweden/EU, Germany, Netherlands, Czechia, US-WA, India, China) — covering the relevant act, certifying standard, enforcement body and Alleima-specific implication for each.
  • Designed a 3-tier methodology framework mapping each site to the right audit strategy: Tier 1 (ISO 50001-certified, fully ENV1-integrated), Tier 2 (active EMS, hybrid audits), Tier 3 (non-certified, simplified ENV1-lite).
  • Specified core audit components common across all tiers: annual energy balance, site walkthrough, EnPI baseline, measure identification, CAPEX/OPEX analysis, M&V, ENV1 upload.
  • Drafted site-specific adaptations per facility (e.g. "formalise EMS docs at Mehsana, pilot ISO 50001-lite", "link ISO audit outputs to ENV1 at Zhenjiang", "shift Scranton from PennTAP audits to hybrid + M&V").
  • Wrote the 2025–2027 phased rollout (year-by-year milestones), governance section (quarterly Corporate Energy Audit Forum, benchmarking dashboards, shared audit library) and ESG-alignment chapter.
  • Authored the M&V chapter: methodology for verifying claimed savings, site-level verified impact summaries, honest data limitations and recommendations for strengthening quantification.

Regulatory matrix

7-country comparison driving the harmonisation choice

The report opens with a comparative table of audit obligations across every jurisdiction where Alleima manufactures. Each cell answers: what's mandatory, which standard, who enforces it, what's the Alleima implication?

  • Sweden / EU — mandatory above 3.8 GWh/yr; ISO 50001 certified audit or ENV1 as the EKL/EED-compliant pathway; Energimyndigheten enforces.
  • Germany — mandatory under EDL-G; ISO 50001 is the common path; BAFA enforces; subsidies available for SMEs.
  • Netherlands — linked to permits via ISO 14001; municipal inspections; energy reviews are indirect.
  • Czech Republic — EU-mandate; ISO 50001 certified; National Energy Authority; site already runs advanced monitoring.
  • United States (WA) — state-specific Climate Commitment Act; EPA / WA Dept. of Ecology; utility-data-driven.
  • India — sector-specific PAT scheme; Bureau of Energy Efficiency; coverage varies by industry.
  • China — national audit guidelines for large users only; provincial authorities; regional variation.

Conclusion drawn: regulations diverge widely, but every regime requires recurring audits, identification of Significant Energy Users (SEUs) and economic assessment of measures. ISO 50001 is the EU lingua-franca but is not universally practical; ENV1 is more scalable.

Methodology

Three-tier framework adapted to site maturity

To balance regulatory diversity and site EMS maturity, the report proposes a three-tiered approach. All tiers share a common cycle and reporting template so cross-site comparisons stay valid.

  • Tier 1 — ISO 50001-certified, EU-regulated: EnMS-driven audits, fully integrated with ENV1, continuous monitoring. Illustrative sites: Sandviken, Chomutov, Werther.
  • Tier 2 — Active EMS but not ISO 50001-certified: hybrid audits — internal reviews + selective third-party verification. Illustrative sites: Mehsana, Zhenjiang.
  • Tier 3 — Non-certified / voluntary contexts: simplified ENV1-lite audits, basic KPIs, external support where needed. Illustrative sites: Kennewick, Scranton.

Core audit components shared across all tiers

  • Annual energy balance (electricity, fuels, compressed air, steam, cooling)
  • Site walkthrough (processes, support systems, maintenance practice)
  • Baseline + EnPIs at process and plant level
  • Measure identification with quantified savings, cost, payback
  • CAPEX/OPEX analysis (payback, ROI, lifecycle cost)
  • Measurement & Verification (M&V) for post-implementation tracking
  • ENV1 upload — findings go into Alleima's corporate system, not just a PDF

Site-specific adaptations

  • Mehsana (IN): formalise EMS documentation, pilot "ISO 50001-lite", automate EnPI collection.
  • Zhenjiang (CN): link ISO audit outputs to ENV1, expand PV monitoring, cover all production lines.
  • Kennewick (US): develop low-cost EnPIs, align state reporting with ENV1, explore DOE voluntary programs.
  • Scranton (US): shift from PennTAP audits to hybrid audits tied to CAPEX and M&V.
  • Venlo (NL): upgrade from ISO 14001 to ISO 50001, automate HVAC controls, standardise reporting cycle.
  • Werther (DE): formalise internal audits, adopt LEEN-network best practices for KPI harmonisation.

Rollout & governance

2025–2027 phased adoption + Corporate Energy Audit Forum

  • 2025 — develop standard templates, upgrade ENV1 modules, pilot ISO 50001-lite at Mehsana.
  • 2026 — expand Tier 2 to Mehsana & Zhenjiang; deliver digital audit training for site leads.
  • 2027 — onboard Tier 3 sites (Scranton, Kennewick); assess ISO 50001 pathways. Deliverables: EnPI handbooks, dashboards, site implementation plans.

Governance mechanisms proposed:

  • Corporate Energy Audit Forum — quarterly sessions for site leads
  • Benchmarking dashboards — real-time EnPI comparison across sites
  • Shared audit library — central repository for templates, best practices, M&V reports

Intent: shift audits from compliance exercises into strategic management tools.

Five key recommendations

What management was asked to adopt

  1. Adopt ENV1 globally as the baseline method for energy mapping.
  2. Use ISO 50001 selectively — only where required or for high-energy users.
  3. Standardise reporting: kWh/MWh, EUR (with local currency in brackets), all sites included in every table.
  4. Clarify terminology: focus on "energy mapping" (ENV1), not generic EnMS audits.
  5. Stronger follow-up on identified measures, with priority for those that pay back in under three years.

Reflection

What I learned about cross-plant industrial energy work

  • Metering is the gate. No EnPI is more credible than the meter it rests on. Half the report's recommendations sequence meter upgrades before chasing energy improvements.
  • Harmonisation beats certification. Pushing every site to ISO 50001 is unrealistic; a tiered framework with one shared internal standard (ENV1) is what actually scales.
  • Cross-plant comparisons are political. Plant-to-plant benchmarks require enough methodology consistency that the regional energy lead trusts the number — and that means an audit-method specification, not just a KPI.
  • Governance is the multiplier. A quarterly forum + a shared library does more long-term work than any single audit cycle.
  • The decision comes before the data. The most useful filter I learned: "What decision will this indicator support?" If the answer is unclear, the indicator is not worth the effort.

Transferable value

Why this experience matters

The 39-page report translated Sweden's EKL/EED model into a tiered framework that fits sites with very different legal and technical maturity. The methodology — boundary, baseline, drivers, residual diagnostics, metering readiness, M&V, governance — is the same one that anchors the EnPI Toolkit and the EU ETS Calculator elsewhere in the portfolio. The role demonstrates how I turn industrial energy context into practical measurement, KPI and decision-support structures for both technical and management audiences.
